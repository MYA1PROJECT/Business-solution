The pricing decision — why 2 cents below, not matching
Three options we considered
❌ Option A — Do nothing
Keep prices as-is. Continue losing 38% of customers to SaveMore on price perception. Foot traffic continues declining. Revenue erosion accelerates.
⚠ Option B — Match SaveMore exactly
Price parity on 7 staples. Neutralises the price objection but gives customers no reason to actively prefer FreshMart. Traffic recovers slowly. No competitive advantage created.
✓ Option C — Price 2 cents below SaveMore (recommended)
FreshMart becomes the cheaper option on anchor items. Customers who were comparing now actively choose FreshMart. Word of mouth shifts. New customers acquired, not just retained.
Why 2 cents is enough
Customers do not calculate exact savings. They form a perception — "FreshMart is cheaper" or "SaveMore is cheaper." Two cents on milk is enough to flip that perception completely. The customer tells their neighbour. The neighbour tells two people. The word-of-mouth effect multiplies the impact of a 2-cent decision across thousands of shoppers.

Why basket size jumps from $41 to $67
They came in for milk and eggs
The staple purchase is the reason to visit. But once inside a well-stocked, comfortable store, customers buy what they see. Impulse categories — snacks, ready meals, seasonal items — are bought in-store, not planned.
They trust FreshMart's produce now
Customers who previously bought vegetables elsewhere now buy them at FreshMart because they're already here. FreshMart's produce is genuinely better. Once customers are in the store regularly, that quality advantage gets discovered.
Loyalty replaces split shopping
Today customers split their shop — staples at SaveMore, fresh at FreshMart. Once FreshMart wins on staple price, the split shop ends. One store gets the entire weekly basket instead of half of it.

The business case in plain language
FreshMart spent 18 months losing customers over 2 cents on a gallon of milk. The fix costs $47,000 per year in margin. It returns $3.4 million in revenue. Customers do not leave a store because milk is 2 cents more expensive — they leave because they believe the store is expensive. Flip that belief with the right 7 items and they come back. And when they come back, they do not buy just milk. They buy everything.
